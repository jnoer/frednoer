Three pedals the only way for stick racers

With the odds stacked so heavily against them, you may be tempted to ask stick-shift racers in NHRA Stock and Super Stock why they even bother running a manual transmission. Go ahead and ask, but don't be surprised if you're met with a blank stare, indicating they may not even understand the question, let alone why you're asking.

That is, they never would consider running an automatic, reducing the number of pedals in their cars from three to two. Labeling them "diehard" is understating their intensity - and their love of a challenge.

"A stick is the only way to go," said Jason McCormack, 37, of Columbiaville, Mich. He began NHRA Stock racing in 1998, and since 2002 he has run an A/S 1966 Plymouth Belvedere with a 600-h.p. Division 3 S/S racers among best in competitive Indy field

Just as practically every aspect of drag racing has changed dramatically in the last 30 years, so has running a stick-shift car in Stock and Super Stock.

How well Jeff Colvert knows. The 57-year-old resident of Arkadelphia, Ark., has been involved in the sport 40 years, and he always has raced stick cars except for half a season when he tried an automatic transmission. He often sets national records, wins class and goes rounds.

The current car of Colvert, who has owned and operated JC Racing CNC Cylinder Heads in Arkadelphia the last five years, is an NHRA Super Stock '69 Mustang notchback he has raced since 1985. The car runs primarily in SS/F and set the national record in October at 9.29 at 143.63.
